# U.S. Passports: Applying and Renewing

Illustrative sample data for a demo — verify with the official agency; not legal, tax, or financial advice.

This guide explains, in general terms, how getting and renewing a United States passport usually works. Passports are issued by the U.S. Department of State. Rules, fees, processing times, and accepted document lists can change, so always confirm the current details on the official passport website before you apply.

## What A Passport Is For

A U.S. passport is the document that proves your U.S. citizenship and identity when you travel internationally. Most international air travel into and out of the country requires a valid passport book. It is also widely accepted as a primary identity document at home.

## Passport Book vs. Passport Card

There are two main forms of the passport, and many people are unsure which they need.

### Passport Book

- The familiar booklet with pages for entry and exit stamps and visas.
- Accepted for **international travel by air, land, and sea**.
- The right choice if you plan to fly internationally at all.

### Passport Card

- A wallet-sized card, similar in size to a driver's license.
- Accepted for **land border crossings and sea ports of entry** between the U.S. and certain neighboring countries and regions.
- **Not valid for international air travel.**
- Often cheaper and more convenient to carry, but more limited.

### Choosing Between Them

- If you might fly internationally, get the **book** (you can request both at once).
- If you only drive or cruise to nearby countries, the **card** may be enough.
- Many applicants request both so they are covered for any kind of trip.

Check the official site for the current fees for each, since the book and card are priced separately and the amounts change over time.

## Who Can Renew By Mail vs. Who Must Apply In Person

This is one of the most common questions, and the answer depends on the condition and history of your most recent passport.

### You Can Generally Renew By Mail If All Of These Are True

- You **have your most recent passport** and it is undamaged (only normal wear).
- It was issued when you were an **adult** (issued at or after a certain age — confirm the exact age cutoff on the official site).
- It was issued **recently enough** (within a defined window of years — confirm the current window officially).
- It was issued in your **current name**, or you can document your name change with the proper legal paperwork (such as a marriage certificate or court order).

When you qualify, you complete the renewal application form designated for renewals and mail it in with your existing passport and the required items. Some renewals may also be available through an official **online renewal** option when it is open; check the official site for current availability and eligibility.

### You Generally Must Apply In Person If Any Of These Apply

- You are getting a passport for the **first time**.
- Your previous passport was **lost, stolen, or significantly damaged**.
- Your previous passport was issued when you were a **minor** (a child).
- Your previous passport was issued **too long ago** to qualify for mail renewal.
- You **cannot submit** your most recent passport for some reason.

In-person applications are submitted at an authorized **acceptance facility** (often a post office, library, or clerk of court) or, in certain situations, at a passport agency. Many facilities ask you to make an appointment, so plan ahead.

## Documents Generally Needed

Exact requirements vary, so treat this as a general checklist and confirm specifics officially.

### Proof of U.S. Citizenship

Examples commonly accepted include:

- A previous U.S. passport (book or card).
- A certified U.S. birth certificate with the required details.
- A Certificate of Naturalization or Certificate of Citizenship.
- A Consular Report of Birth Abroad.

Photocopies are usually not accepted as citizenship evidence; originals or certified copies are typically required, and they may be returned to you separately.

### Proof of Identity

Examples commonly accepted include:

- A valid driver's license or government-issued ID.
- A previous passport.
- A government or military ID.

You generally also need a **photocopy** of the front (and sometimes back) of your identity document.

### Application Form

- First-time and in-person applicants use the standard application form.
- Mail renewals use the dedicated renewal form.
- Forms can usually be completed online and printed, or filled out by hand. Check the official site for the current forms and whether to sign at home or in front of an acceptance agent.

### Passport Photo

See the photo section below for the requirements.

### Payment

- Fees are paid in the form the facility accepts, which differs between mail and in-person submission.
- Do **not** rely on remembered amounts. Check the current fees on the official site, including any separate execution fee for in-person applications and any optional expedite or delivery charges.

## Passport Photo Requirements

A compliant photo is one of the most common reasons applications get delayed, so it is worth getting right.

### General Photo Rules

- **Recent** photo (taken within the last several months) that looks like you currently.
- **Color** photo, printed on photo-quality paper if submitting a printed copy.
- A **plain white or off-white background** with no patterns, objects, or other people.
- Your **full face visible**, facing the camera directly, with a neutral expression or natural smile, and both eyes open.
- **No glasses** in most cases.
- **No hats or head coverings**, except those worn daily for religious purposes (with documentation if requested) or for medical reasons.
- Everyday clothing; **no uniforms** and nothing resembling a uniform.
- The photo must be the **correct size** and show your head at the correct proportion within the frame.

Many drugstores, shipping stores, and some acceptance facilities can take a compliant photo for you. The official site publishes a detailed photo tool and exact dimensions — use it to avoid rejection.

## Routine vs. Expedited Processing

When you apply, you typically choose between standard and faster service.

### Routine (Standard) Processing

- The default service level.
- Takes the standard amount of time the agency is currently quoting.

### Expedited Processing

- Costs an additional fee and moves your application ahead in the queue.
- May be combined with faster delivery shipping for an extra charge.

### Important: Times And Fees Change

Processing times fluctuate with demand throughout the year and can be longer during busy travel seasons. **Do not assume any particular number of weeks.** Always **check the current processing times and fees on the official site** before you book travel. If your trip is very soon, the official site explains the limited options for urgent travel, which may involve scheduling an appointment at a passport agency.

## Practical Tips

- **Apply early.** Build in a comfortable buffer before any planned international trip.
- **Check the expiration rules of your destination.** Many countries require your passport to remain valid for a number of months beyond your travel dates, and some require blank pages. Verify the entry rules for each country you plan to visit.
- **Keep your passport safe.** Replacing a lost or stolen passport requires an in-person application and reporting the loss.
- **Update after a name change.** If your name changed, bring or mail the legal documentation that supports the change.
- **Track your application.** The official site usually offers a status-checking tool.

## Quick Answers

- **How do I renew my passport?** If you still have your most recent adult passport, it is undamaged, and it was issued recently enough and in your current name, you can usually renew by mail (or online when that option is available) using the renewal form. Otherwise, you apply in person at an acceptance facility. Confirm your eligibility and the current forms and fees officially.
- **Book or card?** Get the book for any international air travel; the card only works for land and sea crossings to certain nearby countries.
- **How long does it take?** It varies by season and demand — check the current processing times on the official site and apply early.
