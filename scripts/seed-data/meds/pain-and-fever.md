# Pain and Fever — Common OTC Medications (Reference)

*Illustrative sample data for a demo — educational reference only, not medical advice.
No dosing is given. Read the product's Drug Facts label and ask a pharmacist.*

This page maps common over-the-counter pain and fever relievers between their **generic
names** and **brand names**, names the **drug class**, and describes in general terms
**what the medication is used for**. It does not give doses or compare which is "best" —
that depends on the person and should be discussed with a pharmacist or clinician.

## Two broad families

Most OTC pain/fever relievers fall into one of two groups:

- **Acetaminophen** (a pain reliever and fever reducer that is *not* an NSAID).
- **NSAIDs** (nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs), which reduce pain, fever, *and*
  inflammation. Ibuprofen, naproxen, and aspirin are NSAIDs.

The distinction matters because the two families work differently and have different
general cautions. Anyone deciding between them should check the label and ask a pharmacist.

## Acetaminophen

- **Generic name:** acetaminophen (called *paracetamol* outside the United States).
- **Common brand name:** Tylenol.
- **Drug class:** analgesic (pain reliever) and antipyretic (fever reducer). Not an NSAID.
- **Generally used for:** mild-to-moderate pain (such as headache, muscle aches, toothache,
  menstrual cramps, backache) and reducing fever.
- **General notes:** acetaminophen is also an ingredient in many combination cold, flu, and
  sleep products, so people are often advised to avoid taking more than one acetaminophen-
  containing product at a time. Total daily limits and warnings are on the label.

*Source: [MedlinePlus — Acetaminophen](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a681004.html)*

## Ibuprofen

- **Generic name:** ibuprofen.
- **Common brand names:** Advil, Motrin.
- **Drug class:** NSAID (nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug).
- **Generally used for:** pain, fever, and inflammation — for example headache, dental
  pain, menstrual cramps, muscle and joint aches, and minor arthritis discomfort.
- **General notes:** as an NSAID it carries label cautions related to the stomach and, with
  long-term or high use, other organs. People with certain conditions are told to ask a
  clinician first.

*Source: [MedlinePlus — Ibuprofen](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a682159.html)*

## Naproxen (OTC)

- **Generic name:** naproxen sodium.
- **Common brand name:** Aleve.
- **Drug class:** NSAID.
- **Generally used for:** pain, fever, and inflammation. Compared with ibuprofen, naproxen
  is generally described as longer-acting, which is why the label spacing differs.
- **General notes:** shares the general NSAID cautions noted above.

*Source: [MedlinePlus — Naproxen](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a681029.html)*

## Aspirin

- **Generic name:** aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid).
- **Common brand names:** Bayer, Bufferin (and store brands).
- **Drug class:** NSAID / salicylate. It also has antiplatelet ("blood-thinning") effects.
- **Generally used for:** pain and fever; **low-dose** aspirin is sometimes used under a
  clinician's direction for cardiovascular reasons — that use is a medical decision, not a
  self-care one.
- **General notes:** aspirin is generally **not given to children or teenagers** for viral
  illnesses because of the risk of a rare but serious condition (Reye's syndrome). This is a
  well-known label warning; defer to the label and a clinician.

*Source: [MedlinePlus — Aspirin](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a682878.html)*

## Combination products

Many "cold & flu," "sinus," "PM/nighttime," and "tension headache" products combine one of
the above with other ingredients (for example a decongestant, an antihistamine, or
caffeine). Because the same active ingredient can appear in several products, people are
commonly advised to read every label and avoid doubling up on the same active ingredient.

## How a careful assistant should answer

- "What is the generic name of Tylenol?" → acetaminophen. ✅ (a reference fact)
- "Is Advil an NSAID?" → yes, ibuprofen is an NSAID. ✅
- "How much ibuprofen should I take?" → decline and refer to the label / a pharmacist. 🚫
  (dosing is out of scope for this reference)
- "Which is better for my back pain, X or Y?" → decline to recommend; suggest asking a
  pharmacist or clinician. 🚫 (a personal medical recommendation)
