# Stomach and Digestion — Common OTC Medications (Reference)

*Illustrative sample data for a demo — educational reference only, not medical advice.
No dosing is given. Read the product's Drug Facts label and ask a pharmacist.*

This page maps common over-the-counter digestive medications between **generic** and
**brand** names, names the **drug class**, and describes **what each is generally used for**.
Heartburn and indigestion products are a frequent source of confusion because three
different drug classes are sold side by side and work on different timescales.

## Heartburn and acid reflux — three classes

### Antacids

- **Generic names:** calcium carbonate; magnesium hydroxide; aluminum hydroxide; combinations.
- **Common brand names:** Tums (calcium carbonate), Rolaids, milk of magnesia (magnesium
  hydroxide), Maalox / Mylanta (combinations).
- **Drug class:** antacid (neutralizes stomach acid that is already there).
- **Generally used for:** fast, short-acting relief of occasional heartburn or acid indigestion.

### H2 blockers (H2 receptor antagonists)

- **Generic names:** famotidine; cimetidine.
- **Common brand names:** Pepcid (famotidine), Tagamet (cimetidine).
- **Drug class:** H2 blocker — reduces the amount of acid the stomach produces.
- **Generally used for:** preventing and relieving heartburn; slower to start than an antacid
  but longer-lasting.

### Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs)

- **Generic names:** omeprazole; esomeprazole; lansoprazole.
- **Common brand names:** Prilosec (omeprazole), Nexium (esomeprazole), Prevacid (lansoprazole).
- **Drug class:** proton pump inhibitor — strongly reduces stomach acid production.
- **Generally used for:** frequent heartburn. OTC PPI labels describe a short course rather
  than indefinite daily use; ongoing symptoms are a reason to see a clinician.

## Nausea, motion sickness, and related

### Dimenhydrinate / meclizine

- **Generic names:** dimenhydrinate; meclizine.
- **Common brand names:** Dramamine (dimenhydrinate; "Dramamine Less Drowsy" is meclizine),
  Bonine (meclizine).
- **Drug class:** antihistamines used as antiemetics (anti-nausea) for motion sickness.
- **Generally used for:** preventing or easing motion sickness; these commonly cause drowsiness.

### Bismuth subsalicylate

- **Generic name:** bismuth subsalicylate.
- **Common brand name:** Pepto-Bismol (also Kaopectate in some formulations).
- **Drug class:** antidiarrheal / antacid with a salicylate component.
- **Generally used for:** upset stomach, indigestion, nausea, and mild diarrhea. Because it
  contains a salicylate (aspirin-related) component, the same children/teen viral-illness
  caution noted for aspirin applies — defer to the label and a clinician.

## Diarrhea and constipation

### Loperamide

- **Generic name:** loperamide.
- **Common brand name:** Imodium.
- **Drug class:** antidiarrheal.
- **Generally used for:** slowing down and reducing short-term diarrhea. Labels set firm
  daily limits; exceeding them is dangerous, so dosing belongs to the label, not a chatbot.

### Common laxatives

- **Polyethylene glycol** — brand MiraLAX — an osmotic laxative for occasional constipation.
- **Psyllium** — brand Metamucil — a bulk-forming fiber.
- **Bisacodyl** — brand Dulcolax — a stimulant laxative.
- **Docusate** — brand Colace — a stool softener.

Each works differently; which one suits a given situation is a question for a pharmacist.

## How a careful assistant should answer

- "What's the generic name of Prilosec?" → omeprazole. ✅
- "Is Pepcid a PPI?" → no — famotidine (Pepcid) is an H2 blocker; Prilosec/Nexium are PPIs. ✅
- "How much Imodium can I take?" → don't give a number; point to the label's limit and a
  pharmacist. 🚫
- "I've had heartburn every day for a month, what should I do?" → don't self-diagnose or
  prescribe; recommend seeing a clinician (frequent/persistent symptoms warrant evaluation). 🚫
