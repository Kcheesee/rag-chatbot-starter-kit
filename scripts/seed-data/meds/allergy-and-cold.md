# Allergy and Cold — Common OTC Medications (Reference)

*Illustrative sample data for a demo — educational reference only, not medical advice.
No dosing is given. Read the product's Drug Facts label and ask a pharmacist.*

This page maps common over-the-counter allergy and cold medications between **generic** and
**brand** names, names the **drug class**, and describes **what each is generally used for**.
Cold and allergy aisles are confusing largely because many products are *combinations*, so
the class-by-class breakdown below is the useful part.

## Antihistamines

Antihistamines reduce allergy symptoms such as sneezing, runny nose, itchy or watery eyes,
and hives. They are split into "first-generation" (more likely to cause drowsiness) and
"second-generation" (generally non-drowsy) groups.

### Diphenhydramine

- **Generic name:** diphenhydramine.
- **Common brand name:** Benadryl.
- **Drug class:** first-generation (sedating) antihistamine.
- **Generally used for:** allergy symptoms and itching; because it commonly causes
  drowsiness, the same ingredient appears in many "nighttime" / sleep products.

### Cetirizine

- **Generic name:** cetirizine.
- **Common brand name:** Zyrtec.
- **Drug class:** second-generation (generally non-drowsy) antihistamine.
- **Generally used for:** ongoing allergy symptoms such as hay fever and hives.

### Loratadine

- **Generic name:** loratadine.
- **Common brand name:** Claritin.
- **Drug class:** second-generation (non-drowsy) antihistamine.
- **Generally used for:** allergy symptoms; often chosen when a non-sedating option is wanted.

### Fexofenadine

- **Generic name:** fexofenadine.
- **Common brand name:** Allegra.
- **Drug class:** second-generation (non-drowsy) antihistamine.
- **Generally used for:** seasonal and year-round allergy symptoms.

## Decongestants

Decongestants relieve a stuffy nose and sinus pressure by reducing swelling in nasal
passages.

### Pseudoephedrine

- **Generic name:** pseudoephedrine.
- **Common brand name:** Sudafed (the original "behind-the-counter" formulation).
- **Drug class:** oral decongestant.
- **Generally used for:** nasal and sinus congestion. In the United States it is kept behind
  the pharmacy counter and its sale is tracked, even though no prescription is required.

### Phenylephrine

- **Generic name:** phenylephrine.
- **Common brand names:** appears in many "Sudafed PE" and "-D"/daytime cold products.
- **Drug class:** oral decongestant.
- **Generally used for:** nasal congestion; commonly found in combination cold products on
  the open shelf.

### Oxymetazoline

- **Generic name:** oxymetazoline.
- **Common brand name:** Afrin.
- **Drug class:** topical (nasal-spray) decongestant.
- **Generally used for:** short-term relief of nasal congestion. Labels warn against using
  nasal decongestant sprays for more than a few days in a row because of rebound congestion.

## Cough medications

### Dextromethorphan

- **Generic name:** dextromethorphan (often shown as "DM" on labels).
- **Common brand names:** Robitussin DM, Delsym, and many combination products.
- **Drug class:** cough suppressant (antitussive).
- **Generally used for:** temporarily quieting a dry, hacking cough.

### Guaifenesin

- **Generic name:** guaifenesin.
- **Common brand name:** Mucinex.
- **Drug class:** expectorant.
- **Generally used for:** thinning and loosening mucus so a productive cough can clear it.

## Why combination products cause double-dosing mistakes

A single "cold & flu" box may contain an antihistamine **and** a decongestant **and** a
cough medicine **and** acetaminophen. Because those same ingredients are sold separately,
people are commonly advised to read every active ingredient and avoid taking two products
that share one. (See also the pain-and-fever reference for acetaminophen.)

## How a careful assistant should answer

- "What is the generic name for Zyrtec?" → cetirizine. ✅
- "Is Claritin drowsy or non-drowsy?" → loratadine is a second-generation, generally
  non-drowsy antihistamine. ✅
- "What can I take for my child's cold?" → decline to recommend; refer to a pharmacist or
  pediatrician, since children's products and suitability are a clinical decision. 🚫
- "How often can I use Afrin?" → don't give a schedule; point to the label's warning about
  short-term use and suggest a pharmacist. 🚫
