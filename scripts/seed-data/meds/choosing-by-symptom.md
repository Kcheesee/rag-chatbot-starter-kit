# Choosing by Symptom — An OTC Landscape Guide (Reference)

*Illustrative sample data for a demo — educational reference only, not medical advice.
No dosing is given, and no product is recommended for any individual. Read the product's
Drug Facts label and ask a pharmacist.*

This page is a symptom-oriented map of the **over-the-counter (OTC) landscape**: for each
common symptom it names the relevant drug *classes* (with example generics and brands),
explains in general terms how those classes differ, and points to the detailed reference
pages in this corpus. It is deliberately a *landscape* guide, not a chooser.

Picking the right product for a particular person depends on their **health history, other
medications, age, and pregnancy or breastfeeding status** — so the single most useful thing
this reference can tell you is: **ask a pharmacist or clinician, and read the label.** This
assistant describes the categories; it does not pick one for you, give a dose, or diagnose.

See also the companion reference pages, which carry the generic ↔ brand mappings, drug
classes, and source citations for each medication named below: **pain-and-fever** (acetaminophen
and the NSAIDs), **allergy-and-cold** (antihistamines, decongestants, cough suppressants,
expectorants), **stomach-and-digestion** (antacids, H2 blockers, PPIs, antiemetics,
antidiarrheals, laxatives), and **_disclaimer** (the scope and limits of this whole reference).

## Headache and general aches

**Askable question: "What can I take for a headache?"**

What categories exist:

- **Acetaminophen** (brand Tylenol) — an analgesic/antipyretic that is *not* an NSAID.
- **NSAIDs** such as **ibuprofen** (Advil, Motrin) or **naproxen** (Aleve), and **aspirin**
  (Bayer) — these relieve pain *and* reduce inflammation.

What generally distinguishes them: acetaminophen works on pain and fever but has little
anti-inflammatory effect; NSAIDs add anti-inflammatory action, which is why they are often
discussed for muscle and joint aches, but they carry a different set of label cautions (for
example stomach-related warnings, and the children/teen viral-illness caution for aspirin).
The two families are detailed, with sources, in **pain-and-fever**. Which one is right for a
given person depends on their health history, other medications, age, and pregnancy status —
so they should ask a pharmacist. This reference does not pick.

*Source: [MedlinePlus — Acetaminophen](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a681004.html); [MedlinePlus — Ibuprofen](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a682159.html)*

## Fever

**Askable question: "What kind of medicine brings down a fever?"**

What categories exist:

- **Acetaminophen** (Tylenol) — an antipyretic (fever reducer).
- **NSAIDs** such as **ibuprofen** (Advil) or **naproxen** (Aleve) — also reduce fever.

What generally distinguishes them: both families lower fever; they differ in their other
effects and cautions, exactly as described under headache above and in **pain-and-fever**.
**Aspirin** is generally not given to children or teenagers for viral illnesses because of
the rare but serious risk of Reye's syndrome — a well-known label warning. A high fever, a
fever that lasts, or fever in an infant or young child is a reason to seek care rather than
to self-treat (see *When to seek professional care* below). Which reducer suits a particular
person, and whether one is appropriate at all, is a pharmacist or clinician question — not
one this reference answers.

*Source: [MedlinePlus — Acetaminophen](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a681004.html); [MedlinePlus — Aspirin](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a682878.html)*

## Seasonal allergies (sneezing, runny nose, itchy or watery eyes)

**Askable question: "What's good for itchy eyes and sneezing from allergies?"**

What categories exist:

- **Antihistamines** — split into "first-generation" (more likely to cause drowsiness,
  e.g. **diphenhydramine** / Benadryl) and "second-generation" (generally non-drowsy, e.g.
  **cetirizine** / Zyrtec, **loratadine** / Claritin, **fexofenadine** / Allegra).

What generally distinguishes them: antihistamines target the sneezing, runny nose, and
itchy/watery eyes of allergies. The main practical contrast is drowsiness — first-generation
agents commonly cause it (which is why the same ingredient appears in many nighttime
products), while second-generation agents are generally marketed as non-drowsy. These are laid out, with sources, in
**allergy-and-cold**. Whether an antihistamine is suitable, and which generation fits a given
person's day, depends on their health history, other medications, age, and pregnancy status —
a pharmacist question.

*Source: [MedlinePlus — Cetirizine](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a623043.html); [MedlinePlus — Loratadine](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a697038.html)*

## Nasal and sinus congestion

**Askable question: "What can I take for a stuffy nose / sinus pressure?"**

What categories exist:

- **Oral decongestants** — **pseudoephedrine** (Sudafed, kept behind the pharmacy counter in
  the U.S.) and **phenylephrine** (the "PE" in many shelf cold products).
- **Topical (nasal-spray) decongestants** — **oxymetazoline** (Afrin).

What generally distinguishes them: decongestants reduce swelling in the nasal passages to
relieve stuffiness and sinus pressure — a different job from antihistamines, which is why
"congestion" and "sneezing/itchy eyes" point to different classes. Two notes carried in
**allergy-and-cold**: nasal-spray decongestants come with a label warning against use beyond
a few days (rebound congestion), and in 2024 the FDA proposed removing *oral* phenylephrine
from the OTC monograph on *effectiveness* grounds (the finding does not apply to nasal-spray
phenylephrine). Many cold boxes combine a decongestant with other ingredients, so reading
every active ingredient matters. Which option fits a given person depends on their health
history, other medications, age, and pregnancy status — ask a pharmacist. This reference
does not choose.

*Source: [MedlinePlus — Pseudoephedrine](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a682619.html); [MedlinePlus — Oxymetazoline Nasal Spray](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a608026.html)*

## Cough — dry vs. productive (chesty)

**Askable question: "Is there a difference between cough medicines?"**

What categories exist:

- **Cough suppressants (antitussives)** — **dextromethorphan** ("DM" on labels; Robitussin DM,
  Delsym).
- **Expectorants** — **guaifenesin** (Mucinex).

What generally distinguishes them: the two classes do opposite-sounding jobs, which is the
key landscape point. A **cough suppressant** is generally described for a *dry, hacking*
cough — it quiets the cough reflex. An **expectorant** is generally described for a
*productive (chesty)* cough — it thins and loosens mucus so a cough can clear it. Many
combination products contain both plus a decongestant and/or acetaminophen, so people are
advised to read every active ingredient to avoid doubling up (see **allergy-and-cold** and,
for acetaminophen, **pain-and-fever**).

Whether to quiet a cough or help clear it, and which product suits a given person, depends on
their health and other medications — a pharmacist question, not one this reference settles.

*Source: [MedlinePlus — Dextromethorphan](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a682492.html); [MedlinePlus — Guaifenesin](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a682494.html)*

## Sore throat

**Askable question: "What helps a sore throat?"**

What categories exist:

- **Oral pain relievers / fever reducers** — **acetaminophen** (Tylenol) or an **NSAID** such
  as **ibuprofen** (Advil) or **naproxen** (Aleve), for the general aching discomfort (see
  **pain-and-fever**).
- **Topical soothing products** — medicated lozenges and throat sprays are also sold for
  local, temporary relief; the specific local anesthetics and soothing agents in them vary by
  product, so the Drug Facts label is the reference for what each one contains.

What generally distinguishes them: oral analgesics work on the whole-body ache, while
lozenges and sprays act locally on the throat. A sore throat that is severe, that lasts, that
comes with a high fever, or that makes swallowing or breathing difficult is a reason to see a
clinician rather than self-treat (see *When to seek professional care*), and this reference
cannot tell anyone whether a sore throat is, for example, strep — that is a diagnosis. Which
product fits a given person depends on their health history, other medications, age, and
pregnancy status — ask a pharmacist.

*Source: [MedlinePlus — Acetaminophen](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a681004.html); [MedlinePlus — Ibuprofen](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a682159.html)*

## Heartburn and acid indigestion

**Askable question: "What's the difference between Tums, Pepcid, and Prilosec?"**

What categories exist (three classes, working on different timescales — all detailed in
**stomach-and-digestion**):

- **Antacids** — **calcium carbonate** (Tums), magnesium/aluminum products (Rolaids, milk of
  magnesia, Maalox/Mylanta). They neutralize acid that is already present.
- **H2 blockers** — **famotidine** (Pepcid), **cimetidine** (Tagamet). They reduce how much
  acid the stomach makes.
- **Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs)** — **omeprazole** (Prilosec), **esomeprazole** (Nexium),
  **lansoprazole** (Prevacid). They strongly reduce acid production.

What generally distinguishes them: antacids act fast but briefly; H2 blockers start slower but
last longer; PPIs are aimed at *frequent* heartburn and their OTC labels describe a short
course rather than open-ended daily use. Heartburn that is frequent, persistent, or worsening
is a reason to see a clinician rather than keep self-treating.

Which class fits a given person depends on their health history, other medications, age, and
pregnancy status — a pharmacist question. This reference does not choose among them.

*Source: [MedlinePlus — Calcium Carbonate](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a601032.html); [MedlinePlus — Famotidine](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a687011.html); [MedlinePlus — Omeprazole](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a693050.html)*

## Upset stomach and nausea / motion sickness

**Askable question: "What's used for motion sickness or an upset stomach?"**

What categories exist:

- **Antihistamines used as antiemetics** — **dimenhydrinate** (Dramamine) and **meclizine**
  (Bonine; "Dramamine Less Drowsy"), generally discussed for preventing or easing motion
  sickness; these commonly cause drowsiness.
- **Bismuth subsalicylate** (Pepto-Bismol; also some Kaopectate) — generally discussed for
  upset stomach, indigestion, nausea, and mild diarrhea.

What generally distinguishes them: the motion-sickness antihistamines act on the balance- and
nausea-related pathways and are often taken with travel in mind, whereas bismuth subsalicylate
is a broader "upset stomach" product. Because bismuth subsalicylate contains a salicylate
(aspirin-related) component, the same children/teen viral-illness caution noted for aspirin
applies. Details and sources are in **stomach-and-digestion**. Which option fits a given
person depends on their health history, other medications, age, and pregnancy status — ask a
pharmacist. Persistent vomiting, or nausea with severe pain, warrants medical care.

*Source: [MedlinePlus — Meclizine](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a682548.html); [MedlinePlus — Bismuth Subsalicylate](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a607040.html)*

## Diarrhea

**Askable question: "What can I take for diarrhea?"**

What categories exist:

- **Antidiarrheals** — **loperamide** (Imodium), which slows and reduces short-term diarrhea.
- **Bismuth subsalicylate** (Pepto-Bismol) is also used for mild diarrhea (see above and
  **stomach-and-digestion**).

What generally distinguishes them: loperamide acts on gut motility; bismuth subsalicylate is a
broader upset-stomach product. Loperamide labels set firm daily limits because exceeding them
is dangerous — which is exactly why dosing belongs to the label and a pharmacist, not to a
chatbot. Diarrhea that is severe, bloody, or prolonged, or that comes with high fever or signs
of dehydration, is a reason to seek care. Which option fits a given person depends on their
health history, other medications, age, and pregnancy status — ask a pharmacist.

*Source: [MedlinePlus — Loperamide](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a682280.html); [MedlinePlus — Bismuth Subsalicylate](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a607040.html)*

## Constipation

**Askable question: "Which kind of laxative is there?"**

What categories exist (all detailed in **stomach-and-digestion**):

- **Bulk-forming fiber** — **psyllium** (Metamucil).
- **Osmotic laxatives** — **polyethylene glycol 3350** (MiraLAX).
- **Stool softeners (emollients)** — **docusate** (Colace).
- **Stimulant laxatives** — **bisacodyl** (Dulcolax).

What generally distinguishes them: each class works by a different mechanism — adding bulk,
drawing in water, softening stool, or stimulating the bowel — and they suit different
situations. This is precisely the kind of choice that depends on the person. Which one suits
a given situation depends on health history, other medications, age, and pregnancy status — a
pharmacist question. This reference names the categories; it does not pick one. Constipation
that is persistent, or that comes with severe pain, is a reason to see a clinician.

*Source: [MedlinePlus — Psyllium](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a601104.html); [MedlinePlus — Polyethylene Glycol 3350](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a603032.html); [MedlinePlus — Bisacodyl](https://medlineplus.gov/druginfo/meds/a601027.html)*

## When to seek professional care

OTC products are intended for *minor, short-lived* symptoms. The following general red flags
mean a person should contact a clinician or pharmacist rather than rely on self-care, and this
reference **cannot diagnose** what is causing them:

- Symptoms that are **severe, persistent, worsening, or unusual** for the person.
- A **high fever**, or a fever that **lasts more than a few days**.
- **Trouble breathing**, chest pain, severe or worsening pain, confusion, fainting, signs of
  dehydration, or a sore throat with difficulty swallowing or breathing.
- Symptoms in **infants and young children**, during **pregnancy or breastfeeding**, or in
  someone with a **chronic condition** or who takes **other medications** — these situations
  often change what is safe or appropriate.
- Any suspected **overdose or serious reaction** — contact emergency or poison-control services
  immediately (see **_disclaimer**).

When in doubt, the safe answer is to ask a pharmacist or see a clinician. This reference
describes the OTC landscape; it does not replace a professional's judgment for a specific person.

## What this assistant will and won't do

This is the guardrail, stated plainly:

- ✅ It **explains drug classes** (for example, what an NSAID, an antihistamine, a decongestant,
  an antacid, an H2 blocker, a PPI, an antitussive, an expectorant, or a laxative *is*).
- ✅ It maps **generic ↔ brand** names (for example, ibuprofen ↔ Advil; cetirizine ↔ Zyrtec;
  omeprazole ↔ Prilosec).
- ✅ It describes **what categories are generally used for** at a consumer-education level, as on
  this page.
- 🚫 It does **not give dosing**, frequency, or administration details — those are on the Drug
  Facts label.
- 🚫 It does **not make a personal "what should I take" recommendation** — which product is right
  depends on a person's health history, other medications, age, and pregnancy status, so that
  goes to a **pharmacist**.
- 🚫 It does **not diagnose** — figuring out what is wrong goes to a **clinician**.

In short: this reference tells you what the *landscape* looks like. The personalized
choice, the dose, and the diagnosis belong to a pharmacist or a clinician.
