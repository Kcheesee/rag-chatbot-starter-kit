# Bread Techniques

The hands-on methods that turn a formula into a good loaf. These are general techniques with typical ranges, not a single recipe, so you can apply them across styles.

## Developing Gluten

Gluten is the protein network that traps gas and gives bread its rise and chew. There are several ways to develop it, and they can be combined.

### Kneading

Kneading works the dough mechanically to align and strengthen gluten strands into long, organized strands, giving the dough both extensibility (the ability to stretch) and elasticity (the ability to snap back). By hand, this usually means pressing the dough away with the heel of your palm, folding it back, rotating, and repeating. It typically takes 8-12 minutes by hand or 5-8 minutes in a stand mixer on low-to-medium speed.

Signs of well-kneaded dough: it becomes smooth, elastic, and slightly tacky rather than sticky, and it springs back when pressed.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — What is the windowpane test?](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2022/10/14/what-is-the-windowpane-test-for-bread-dough)*

### No-Knead Method

No-knead breads rely on time instead of muscle. A wet dough is mixed briefly and left to ferment for many hours (often 12-18, until bubbly and roughly doubled). During that long rest, the increased mobility of the proteins in a wet dough lets gentle fermentation develop the gluten on its own. The trade-off is patience for effort, and the results are excellent for rustic, open-crumb loaves.

*Source: [No-knead bread (Jim Lahey method) — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/No-knead_bread)*

### Autolyse

Autolyse is a rest of just flour and water before full mixing, with salt and yeast held back because both have a tightening effect on gluten. (A liquid levain or preferment is the usual exception, since its water is needed to hydrate the flour.) Twenty minutes to an hour is common. The flour fully hydrates, enzymes begin working, and gluten starts forming passively. The payoff is dough that is more extensible, easier to develop, and needs less kneading. Salt and yeast are added after the autolyse.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Using the autolyse method](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2017/09/29/autolyse-sourdough)*

### Stretch-and-Fold

Stretch-and-folds build strength gently during bulk fermentation, especially in wetter doughs that are hard to knead. Wet your hand, grab one side of the dough, stretch it up, and fold it over the center; rotate the bowl and repeat on all four sides. A set of folds takes seconds.

Typically you do 2-4 sets spaced 20-45 minutes apart in the first part of bulk. The dough noticeably tightens and smooths from set to set. This is the workhorse technique for high-hydration and sourdough breads.

### Coil Folds and Lamination

Two variations on folding for slack doughs. A coil fold lifts the dough from the middle and lets the ends tuck under, building tension without degassing much. Lamination stretches the dough thin on a wet surface and folds it back up, which distributes strength and any add-ins evenly. Both are gentler alternatives to kneading for very wet doughs.

### The Windowpane Test

The windowpane test checks whether gluten is sufficiently developed. Take a piece about the size of a ping-pong ball and stretch it slowly between your fingers. If it stretches thin enough to let light through without tearing, like a pane of glass, the gluten is well developed. If it tears almost as soon as you start stretching, it needs more kneading or folding (or more rest). Keep in mind it is a guide to where the dough is in its development, not strictly pass/fail: doughs built mostly through long fermentation or folds keep gaining strength as they rest.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — What is the windowpane test?](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2022/10/14/what-is-the-windowpane-test-for-bread-dough)*

## Bulk Fermentation in Practice

Bulk fermentation is the first rise of the whole dough mass. Beyond producing gas, it builds flavor and strength. Judge it by change, not strictly by the clock: look for a dough that has risen meaningfully, feels airy and domed, and shows bubbles. Temperature drives the pace, so a warm kitchen shortens bulk and a cool one lengthens it.

A common pitfall is watching the clock instead of the dough. The same recipe can take very different times depending on temperature. Aim to read the dough's signs rather than trusting a fixed time.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Bulk fermentation, explained](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2019/07/22/bulk-fermentation)*

## Shaping and Tension

Shaping gives the loaf its final form and, crucially, builds surface tension. A taut skin on the outside of the dough helps it hold its shape and rise up rather than spreading out flat.

### Pre-Shaping

Before final shaping, the dough is often gently formed into a loose round and left to rest 15-30 minutes (the bench rest). This relaxes the gluten so the final shape is easier and the dough does not fight back.

### Building Tension

During final shaping you fold and roll the dough so the outer surface is stretched smooth and tight, then seal the seam. For a round (boule) you drag it across the counter to tighten the skin; for a batard or sandwich loaf you roll it up into a taut cylinder. Good tension is the difference between a tall, well-domed loaf and a flat, spreading one.

### Using a Banneton

Shaped dough often proofs in a banneton (a proofing basket), which supports the loaf, wicks a little moisture from the surface, and leaves an attractive spiral or grid pattern. Flour the basket well, place the dough seam-side up, and it will turn out seam-side down for scoring.

## Proofing

Proofing is the final rise after shaping. The dough relaxes and inflates one last time before baking. Getting proof right is one of the biggest levers on a good crumb and good oven spring.

### Room-Temperature vs. Cold Proof

- **Room temperature**: faster, often 1-3 hours depending on warmth and leavening.
- **Cold retard (refrigerator)**: slower, often 8-16 hours or more. It develops deeper flavor, firms the dough so it scores cleanly, and lets you bake on your own schedule.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — What is proofing bread?](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2023/08/31/proofing-bread)*

### The Poke Test

The poke test judges proof readiness. Lightly flour a fingertip and gently press the dough about a centimeter (roughly an inch) deep, then watch how it springs back.

- **Springs back quickly and fully**: underproofed, give it more time.
- **Springs back slowly and leaves a slight indent**: ready to bake.
- **Does not spring back, indent stays, dough may feel slack or deflate**: overproofed.

For cold-proofed dough, judge it cold and remember it will not visibly spring back as fast as warm dough. Aim to proof in the low-to-mid 70s F (around 22-25 C); cooler slows things and warmer speeds them up.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — What is proofing bread?](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2023/08/31/proofing-bread)*

## Scoring

Scoring is cutting the surface of the dough just before baking, usually with a razor blade or lame. It is both functional and decorative.

### Why Scoring Matters

As the loaf springs in the oven, it needs somewhere to expand. A deliberate score gives the gases a controlled place to break out, so the loaf opens where you want rather than tearing randomly along a weak seam. King Arthur's own baking trials found unscored loaves were restricted, with tighter, denser interiors, while scored loaves gained greater volume and a more open crumb. Without scoring, a lean loaf often bursts at the side.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Baking trials: how much does scoring impact bread?](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2024/05/30/scoring-bread-dough)*

### How to Score

Use a sharp blade and a confident, swift motion. For a big ear (the raised flap of crust), hold the blade at a shallow angle and make one decisive cut. Depth depends on the dough: room-temperature loaves like baguettes may need only about 1/4 inch (roughly half a centimeter), while chilled sourdough often wants a deeper cut, up to 1/2 to 3/4 inch (about 1.5-2 cm), to fully release its expansion. Decorative patterns are usually shallower. Cold dough scores far more cleanly than warm, slack dough.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Baking trials: how much does scoring impact bread?](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2024/05/30/scoring-bread-dough)*

## Steam

Steam in the early bake is essential for lean, crusty breads.

### What Steam Does

A moist environment during the oven-spring window, roughly the first 15-25 minutes, keeps the crust soft and pliable so the loaf can expand fully. It also gelatinizes the surface starches, which gives a thin, glossy, crackly crust with good color and blistering. Without steam, the surface dries and sets too early, limiting rise and leaving the crust pale and dull. But do not over-steam for the whole bake: once the loaf is set, lingering moisture leads to a thick, rubbery crust.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Baking bread with steam](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2017/02/28/baking-bread-with-steam)*

### How to Create Steam at Home

- **Dutch oven**: the simplest and most reliable method. The lid traps the dough's own moisture; remove it partway through to dry and color the crust.
- **Tray of water or ice**: place a preheated pan on a lower shelf and add boiling water or ice when you load the loaf.
- **Spritzing**: misting the oven walls (not the loaf) a few times in the first minute adds a quick burst of steam.

Vent the steam (or remove the lid) for the second half of the bake so the crust can crisp and brown.

## Baking Temperatures

Lean, crusty breads bake hot. Enriched breads bake cooler so the sugar and fat do not burn before the inside is done.

### General Ranges

- **Lean rustic loaves and sourdough**: roughly 220-250 C (425-480 F); many recipes bake around 450-475 F, often starting at the high end with steam and finishing slightly lower once vented.
- **Sandwich and pan breads**: roughly 190-220 C (375-425 F).
- **Enriched breads (brioche, milk bread)**: roughly 165-190 C (325-375 F), lower because their sugar and fat brown quickly.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Extra-tangy sourdough bread](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/recipes/extra-tangy-sourdough-bread-recipe)*

### Judging Doneness

Color is a good first signal, but a reliable test is internal temperature. Lean, crusty breads are generally done around 96-100 C (205-210 F) internal; soft and enriched breads are commonly pulled around 88-91 C (190-195 F), with King Arthur using 190 F as a benchmark for soft loaves and rolls. (For very lean crusty loaves, crust color is often the better guide, since the interior plateaus near water's boiling point.) A deep golden-brown crust and a hollow sound when you tap the base are classic supporting signs.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Using a thermometer with yeast bread](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2017/04/07/using-a-thermometer-with-yeast-bread)*  
*Source: [King Arthur Baking — How to tell if bread is done](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2023/05/31/how-to-tell-if-bread-is-done)*

### Preheating

Preheat thoroughly, including any baking stone, steel, or Dutch oven, for at least 30-45 minutes. A fully hot oven and hot baking surface drive strong oven spring; a half-heated oven gives weak, flat loaves.

## Cooling

Resist cutting a fresh loaf. The interior is still setting and finishing as it cools, and slicing too early lets steam escape before the crumb has set and can leave it pasty. Cool lean loaves on a wire rack for at least an hour, often 1-2 hours (and much longer for dense rye, which can want most of a day) before slicing.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — How to tell if bread is done](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2023/05/31/how-to-tell-if-bread-is-done)*
