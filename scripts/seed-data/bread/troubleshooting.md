# Bread Troubleshooting

A problem-by-problem guide to the most common home breadmaking issues. Each section names the likely causes and the fixes, so you can match a symptom to a remedy.

## Crumb Problems

### Why Is My Crumb Dense or Tight?

A dense, tight crumb with small or no holes is the most common complaint. It usually means the dough was underfermented, weak, or handled in a way that knocked the gas out.

**Likely causes:**

- **Underproofing or short bulk fermentation.** The yeast or starter did not have enough time or warmth to produce gas. This is the number one cause.
- **Weak or inactive leavening.** Old yeast, or a sourdough starter that is not lively and at peak.
- **Underdeveloped gluten.** Without enough kneading or folding, the dough cannot trap gas.
- **Too little water.** Low hydration tends toward a tighter crumb.
- **Degassing during shaping.** Pressing too hard or shaping too aggressively crushes the bubbles.
- **Too much heavy flour.** A high proportion of whole wheat or rye naturally makes a denser loaf.

**Fixes:**

- Extend bulk fermentation and judge by the dough (risen, airy, jiggly), not the clock.
- Test your yeast or feed your starter and use it when it is bubbly and at peak.
- Develop the gluten more with kneading or additional stretch-and-folds.
- Raise hydration slightly if your flour can handle it.
- Shape more gently to preserve the gas you built.
- Keep the dough warmer (around 24-26 C / 75-78 F) so fermentation moves along.

### Why Are There Big Holes Only Near the Top (Tunneling)?

Large irregular tunnels just under the crust, with a tighter base, usually point to uneven shaping or trapped pockets. Degas more evenly during shaping, seal the dough well, and avoid leaving large air pockets when you form the loaf. Slightly under- or overproofing can also contribute.

## Rising Problems

### Why Didn't My Dough Rise?

If the dough barely moves during bulk or proof, the leavening or the environment is the problem.

**Likely causes:**

- **Dead or expired yeast.** Yeast loses potency with age and heat.
- **Yeast killed by hot water.** Water above roughly 50 C (120 F) can damage or kill yeast.
- **Cold environment.** A cold kitchen dramatically slows fermentation; the dough may be working, just slowly.
- **Sluggish sourdough starter.** A starter that is young, hungry, or kept cold will not leaven strongly.
- **Too much salt, or salt in direct contact with yeast.** Excess salt inhibits yeast.

**Fixes:**

- Proof a little yeast in warm sugar water; if it foams in 10 minutes it is alive.
- Use lukewarm water, not hot.
- Move the dough somewhere warmer (an oven with just the light on works well).
- Feed your starter on a regular schedule and use it at peak activity.
- Give it more time; many doughs rise eventually if the leavening is alive.

### My Dough Rose Then Collapsed. What Happened?

A dough that rises beautifully then deflates has usually overproofed: the gluten was stretched past what it could hold and the structure gave out. Shorten the proof, build more strength during bulk with folds, and use the poke test to catch it at the right moment. See also the overproofing section below.

## Texture Problems

### Why Is the Inside Gummy or Doughy?

A gummy, sticky, or pasty interior feels undercooked even when the crust looks fine.

**Likely causes:**

- **Cut too soon.** The crumb sets as the loaf cools; slicing hot releases steam and leaves it gummy. This is extremely common.
- **Underbaked.** Pulled from the oven before the interior reached temperature.
- **Underfermented dough.** Underproofed loaves can bake up dense and gummy.
- **Very high hydration without enough bake time.** Wet doughs need a longer, thorough bake.

**Fixes:**

- Cool completely before slicing, at least an hour for lean loaves (longer for rye).
- Bake longer and check internal temperature: around 96-100 C (205-210 F) for lean breads.
- Make sure bulk and proof are complete before baking.
- For wet doughs, extend the bake and consider a slightly lower final temperature to dry the interior without burning the crust.

## Oven Spring and Crust Problems

### Why Did I Get No Oven Spring?

Oven spring is the dramatic rise in the first minutes of baking. Little or none means the loaf could not expand.

**Likely causes:**

- **Overproofing.** An overproofed loaf has no reserve gas left to expand and may spread instead of rising.
- **Underdeveloped gluten or weak structure.** Nothing to hold the expanding gas.
- **Not enough steam.** A crust that sets too early physically stops expansion.
- **Oven not hot enough, or not preheated.** Weak initial heat means weak spring.
- **No scoring, or scoring too shallow.** The loaf has no controlled place to open.

**Fixes:**

- Catch the proof a touch early so there is gas in reserve for the oven.
- Build strength with kneading or folds and shape with good tension.
- Add steam for the first 10-20 minutes (a Dutch oven is the easiest way).
- Preheat fully, including the stone or Dutch oven, for 30-45 minutes.
- Score decisively, about a centimeter deep, so the loaf can open.

### Why Is My Crust Pale?

A loaf that stays blond and dull usually lacks heat, sugar, or steam.

**Likely causes:**

- **Oven temperature too low** or the loaf pulled too early.
- **Too much steam, too long.** Steam should be vented for the second half so the crust can color.
- **Underfermented dough.** Fermentation consumes and develops the sugars that brown; too little leaves little to caramelize.
- **No sugar in a lean dough that needs more color** (less common; usually one of the above).

**Fixes:**

- Bake hotter and longer, and bake until the crust is genuinely deep golden, not just set.
- Remove the Dutch oven lid (or stop adding steam) partway through to dry and color the crust.
- Make sure bulk fermentation is complete so there is sugar to brown.

### Why Is the Bottom Burnt?

A scorched base while the rest is fine points to too much direct bottom heat.

**Likely causes:**

- **Loaf too close to the bottom element or a very hot stone.**
- **Oven runs hot or has hot spots.**
- **Dark or thin pan that conducts heat aggressively.**

**Fixes:**

- Move the loaf to a higher rack.
- Double up baking sheets or slide a second tray underneath to insulate the base.
- Lower the temperature slightly and check your oven with a separate thermometer.
- For Dutch ovens, a sheet of parchment or a trivet under the dough helps.

## Flavor Problems

### Why Is My Sourdough Too Sour?

Excessive sourness comes from too much acid production, usually from long or warm fermentation that favors the bacteria.

**Likely causes:**

- **Very long fermentation, especially a long warm bulk or a multi-day cold retard.**
- **A starter maintained in a way that builds high acidity** (infrequent feeding, kept warm, low feeding ratios).
- **High proportion of whole grain or rye, which ferments fast and tangy.**

**Fixes:**

- Shorten fermentation, or do the cold retard for less time.
- Feed your starter more often and with larger flour ratios to keep it milder.
- Use a younger, sweeter levain rather than one past peak.
- Cooler bulk fermentation favors yeast over acid-producing bacteria, giving a milder flavor.

### Why Is My Bread Bland or Flavorless?

Flat flavor usually means fermentation was too short or too fast. Lengthen fermentation (a cold overnight proof adds a lot of flavor), reduce the amount of yeast so the rise is slower, and make sure you are using enough salt (around 2% of flour weight). A preferment or a portion of whole grain flour also deepens flavor.

## Proofing Diagnosis

### Overproofed vs. Underproofed: How Do I Tell?

These two opposite problems can look similar to a beginner, so it helps to compare them directly.

**Underproofed dough:**

- Springs back quickly and fully on the poke test.
- Feels tight and firm, not very airy.
- Bakes up dense, may burst or tear in unexpected places, and can have a tight crumb.
- **Fix:** give it more time and warmth before baking.

**Overproofed dough:**

- Does not spring back on the poke test; the indent stays, or the dough deflates.
- Feels slack, very loose, possibly sticky, may have a sour smell.
- Bakes flat with little oven spring, may collapse, and can have a coarse, fragile crumb.
- **Fix:** in mild cases, gently reshape to rebuild a little tension and bake right away; otherwise shorten the proof next time and use the poke test to catch the window.

**Well-proofed dough** sits between the two: the poke test springs back slowly and leaves a slight indent, and the dough feels airy but still holds its shape.
