# Bread Glossary

Concise definitions of the terms a home baker runs into most. Each entry is self-contained so it can answer a "what is X?" question on its own.

## Autolyse

A rest period in which flour and water are mixed and left to sit before salt and yeast are incorporated (both are held back because they tighten gluten; a liquid levain is the usual exception). Usually 20 minutes to an hour. The flour hydrates and gluten begins to form passively, producing a more extensible dough that needs less kneading.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Using the autolyse method](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2017/09/29/autolyse-sourdough)*

## Baker's Percentage

A way of expressing a bread formula where every ingredient is given as a percentage of the total flour weight, and flour is always 100%. It makes recipes easy to scale and compare. For example, 70% water means 700 g of water per 1000 g of flour.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — What is baker's math?](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2022/04/28/what-is-bakers-math-and-how-can-i-use-it-in-my-everyday-bread-baking)*

## Banneton

A proofing basket, often made of cane or rattan, that supports a shaped loaf during its final rise. It helps the dough hold its shape, wicks a little surface moisture, and leaves a decorative spiral or grid pattern on the crust.

## Batard

An oblong, torpedo-shaped loaf, longer than a round boule but shorter and plumper than a baguette. A common shape for sourdough and rustic breads.

## Bench Rest

A short rest (often 15-30 minutes) after pre-shaping and before final shaping. It relaxes the gluten so the dough is easier to shape and does not fight back or tear.

## Boule

A round, ball-shaped loaf. The name is French for "ball." One of the simplest and most common artisan loaf shapes.

## Bulk Fermentation

The first and longest rise, in which the entire mass of dough ferments together before being divided and shaped. Gas builds, the dough gains strength and volume, and flavor develops. It is best judged by the dough's signs (risen, smoother, jiggly, bubbly) rather than the clock. Often shortened to "bulk."

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Bulk fermentation, explained](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2019/07/22/bulk-fermentation)*

## Cold Retard

Slowing fermentation by placing the dough (usually after shaping) in the refrigerator for many hours. It develops deeper flavor, firms the dough for cleaner scoring, and lets the baker control timing. Also called cold proofing or retarding.

## Crumb

The interior texture of a baked loaf: the structure of holes, walls, and softness inside the crust. An "open crumb" has large irregular holes; a "tight" or "close crumb" has small, even holes. Distinct from the crust, which is the outer surface.

## Crust

The outer, browned, firmer surface of a baked loaf. Its character (thin and crackly, thick and chewy, soft) depends on hydration, steam, baking temperature, and bake time.

## Dutch Oven

A heavy lidded pot, often cast iron, used to bake bread at home. The lid traps the dough's own steam during the early bake, which mimics a professional steam-injected oven and produces strong oven spring and a crisp crust. Remove the lid partway through so the crust can dry, color, and crisp.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Baking bread with steam](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2017/02/28/baking-bread-with-steam)*

## Ear

The raised, lifted flap of crust that forms along a score line during oven spring. A prominent ear is a sign of a well-developed, well-scored, properly proofed loaf.

## Enriched Dough

Dough that includes fat, sugar, eggs, and/or dairy in addition to the basic flour, water, salt, and leavening. Examples include brioche, milk bread, and challah. Enriched doughs are softer and richer, ferment more slowly, and brown faster, so they bake at lower temperatures.

## Fermentation

The process by which yeast and bacteria consume sugars in the dough and produce carbon dioxide (which leavens), along with alcohol and acids (which flavor). Fermentation drives both rise and taste in bread.

## Gluten

The elastic protein network formed when the wheat proteins glutenin and gliadin are hydrated and worked and link together. Gluten traps the gas produced by fermentation, giving bread its structure, rise, and chew. Higher-protein flour forms a stronger network; developing gluten well is central to good bread.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Protein percentage in flour](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2023/09/25/protein-percentage)*

## Hydration

The ratio of water weight to flour weight in a dough, expressed as a percentage (for example, 750 g water with 1000 g flour is 75%). Higher hydration gives a wetter, slacker dough and tends toward a more open crumb; lower hydration gives a firmer dough and a tighter crumb.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Bread hydration, explained](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2023/01/11/bread-hydration)*

## Lame

A handled razor blade used to score the surface of dough just before baking. Its sharp edge makes clean, decisive cuts that control where the loaf expands.

## Lamination

A folding technique for very wet doughs in which the dough is stretched thin on a wet surface and folded back up. It builds and distributes strength and evenly disperses any add-ins like seeds or fruit.

## Leavening

Any agent that produces gas to make bread rise. In bread this is usually biological (commercial yeast or a sourdough culture), as opposed to the chemical leaveners (baking soda, baking powder) used in quick breads.

## Levain

A portion of sourdough starter built up (fed) shortly before mixing, sized and timed for a specific dough. In everyday use "levain" and "starter" overlap, but strictly the levain is the off-shoot you prepare for a particular bake while the starter is the mother culture you maintain.

## Open Crumb

A crumb structure with large, irregular, glossy holes, prized in rustic sourdough and ciabatta. It results from high hydration, good gluten development, strong fermentation, and gentle shaping.

## Oven Spring

The rapid expansion of a loaf in the first minutes of baking, before the crust sets. It is driven by trapped gases expanding, water turning to steam, and a final burst of yeast activity in the heat. Good oven spring depends on proper proofing, structure, steam, and a hot oven.

## Poke Test

A way to judge whether dough is properly proofed. You gently press a floured fingertip about an inch into the dough and watch how it rebounds. Quick full spring-back means underproofed; a slow rebound leaving a slight indent means ready; no rebound (the indent stays) means overproofed.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — What is proofing bread?](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2023/08/31/proofing-bread)*

## Pre-Shape

A gentle initial shaping (often into a loose round) done before the bench rest and final shaping. It organizes the dough and builds a little tension, making the final shape cleaner.

## Preferment

A portion of flour, water, and a small amount of leavening mixed ahead of time and fermented before being added to the final dough. Examples include poolish, biga, and sponge. Preferments deepen flavor and improve texture and keeping quality.

## Proofing

The final rise of shaped dough before baking, during which it relaxes and inflates one last time. Can be done at room temperature or as a cold retard. Getting proof right strongly affects crumb and oven spring. (Note: "proofing" can also loosely mean testing whether yeast is alive.)

## Scoring

Cutting the surface of shaped dough just before baking, usually with a lame or razor. Scoring gives expanding gases a controlled place to escape so the loaf opens predictably rather than bursting; King Arthur's baking trials found unscored loaves were restricted with a tighter crumb. It also creates decorative patterns and the ear.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Baking trials: how much does scoring impact bread?](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2024/05/30/scoring-bread-dough)*

## Sourdough Starter

A living culture of wild yeast and lactic acid bacteria kept in a mixture of flour and water and fed regularly. It leavens bread naturally, without commercial yeast, and contributes tangy, complex flavor from the lactic and acetic acids the bacteria produce. Also called the mother or, informally, the starter.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Sourdough baking guide](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/learn/guides/sourdough)*

## Stretch-and-Fold

A gentle technique for building dough strength during bulk fermentation, used especially for wet doughs. You stretch one side of the dough up and fold it over the center, rotating around the bowl. Several sets spaced apart strengthen the gluten without traditional kneading.

## Windowpane Test

A check for gluten development. A small piece of dough (about ping-pong-ball size) is stretched thin between the fingers; if it forms a translucent membrane that lets light through without tearing, the gluten is well developed. If it tears almost immediately, the dough needs more work or rest. It is a guide to development stage, not strictly pass/fail.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — What is the windowpane test?](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2022/10/14/what-is-the-windowpane-test-for-bread-dough)*

## Yeast

A single-celled fungus that ferments sugars and leavens bread. Commercial baking yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) comes as active dry, instant, or fresh; modern active dry and instant can both be mixed straight into the dough, with instant a touch faster. It works faster and more predictably than a wild sourdough culture but contributes less complex flavor.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Active dry vs. instant yeast](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2022/08/15/active-dry-versus-instant-yeast)*
