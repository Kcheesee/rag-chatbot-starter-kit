# Making Bread — A Beginner's Walkthrough

So you want to actually *make* a loaf? This is the friendly, start-to-finish version.
It complements the deeper dives in **fundamentals**, **techniques**, and
**troubleshooting** — here we just walk the whole path once, in order, with what to
look for at each step. (This is the open, free-flow knowledge base, so ask broadly!)

## What you need

A basic lean loaf is just four things: **flour, water, salt, and yeast**. Bakers talk
about the proportions in *baker's percentages* — every ingredient as a percentage of the
flour weight. A simple, reliable starting point for a everyday loaf is roughly:

- **Flour:** 100% (the reference; e.g. 500 g)
- **Water:** ~65% (e.g. 325 g) — this is the "hydration"
- **Salt:** ~1.8–2% (e.g. 9–10 g)
- **Yeast:** ~1% instant, or a bit more active dry (e.g. 5 g)

Weighing in grams is far more consistent than measuring cups. See **fundamentals** for
how hydration and baker's math work.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — What is baker's math?](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2022/04/28/what-is-bakers-math-and-how-can-i-use-it-in-my-everyday-bread-baking)*

## The process at a glance

Mix → knead/develop → bulk ferment (first rise) → divide & shape → final proof →
bake → cool. Most of the clock time is the dough resting, not you working.

## Step 1 — Mix

Combine the flour, water, salt, and yeast until no dry flour remains and you have a
shaggy, cohesive mass. Let it sit 10–20 minutes first (an *autolyse*, flour + water
only, with salt and yeast held back) if you want easier kneading and better structure —
see **techniques**.

## Step 2 — Knead / develop the gluten

Kneading aligns the gluten into a stretchy network that traps gas. Knead until the dough
is smooth and passes the **windowpane test** — a small piece stretches thin enough to see
light through without tearing. Prefer not to knead? Time does it for you — see the
no-knead shortcut below.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — What is the windowpane test?](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2022/10/14/what-is-the-windowpane-test-for-bread-dough)*

## Step 3 — Bulk fermentation (the first rise)

The dough rests and ferments as a single mass — typically until it's noticeably puffy and
roughly doubled, often around 1–2 hours at room temperature (cooler kitchens take
longer). This is where flavor and strength build. Judge by *how the dough looks and
feels*, not the clock: it should be domed, jiggly, and airy.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Understanding bulk fermentation](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2019/07/22/bulk-fermentation)*

## Step 4 — Divide and shape

Gently turn the dough out, divide if making more than one loaf, and shape it — creating
surface **tension** by pulling the dough taut over itself. Tension is what gives a loaf
its height and a clean rise instead of spreading flat. (Shaping detail lives in
**techniques**.)

## Step 5 — Final proof

The shaped loaf rests again until it's ready to bake. Use the **poke test**: lightly press
the dough; it's ready when the dent springs back slowly and only partway. Springs back
instantly = underproofed (give it more time); stays fully dented = overproofed (bake now).

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Proofing bread](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2023/08/31/proofing-bread)*

## Step 6 — Bake

Lean, crusty loaves bake hot — roughly **220–245 °C (425–475 °F)** — often with **steam**
for the first part of the bake so the crust stays soft long enough to expand ("oven
spring"). Don't judge doneness by color alone: the most reliable test is **internal
temperature** — lean breads are generally done around **88–96 °C (190–205 °F)**, softer
enriched breads a touch lower. (Ranges and steam methods are in **techniques**.)

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — How to tell if bread is done](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2023/05/31/how-to-tell-if-bread-is-done)*

## Step 7 — Cool (don't skip it)

Let the loaf cool on a rack before slicing — ideally an hour for a lean loaf. The interior
is still finishing ("setting") as it cools; cutting in early gives a gummy, squished crumb.

## The no-knead shortcut

Short on time or effort? A no-knead loaf trades active kneading for a long, slow rest.
King Arthur's classic version: stir flour, water, salt, and yeast into a *very sticky,
rough dough* (no kneading), let it rise about 2 hours at room temperature, then
refrigerate it for anywhere from 2 hours up to about a week (longer = tangier). When you
want bread, pull off a piece, shape it roughly, let it rise ~60 minutes, slash the top,
and bake hot (~230 °C / 450 °F) with steam — or in a lidded Dutch oven, which traps the
dough's own steam and needs no separate steam pan. It's the easiest possible entry point.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — No-Knead Crusty White Bread](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/recipes/no-knead-crusty-white-bread-recipe)*

## A first-loaf summary

1. Mix 500 g flour, ~325 g water, ~10 g salt, ~5 g instant yeast into a shaggy dough.
2. Knead to a smooth, windowpane-passing dough (or skip and go no-knead).
3. Bulk ferment ~1–2 hours until puffy and ~doubled.
4. Shape with tension; final proof until the poke test passes.
5. Bake hot with steam to a deep golden crust and ~93 °C (200 °F) interior.
6. Cool fully before slicing.

If something goes sideways — dense crumb, no rise, gummy interior — head to
**troubleshooting**, which lists the usual cause-and-fix for each.
