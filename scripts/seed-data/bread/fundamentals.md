# Bread Fundamentals

A practical primer on the building blocks of home breadmaking: how bakers think about ratios, what each ingredient does, and the high-level path dough takes from a bowl of flour to a finished loaf.

## Baker's Percentages

Baker's percentages are a simple way to describe a bread formula so it scales cleanly and is easy to compare across recipes. Every ingredient is expressed as a percentage of the total flour weight, and the flour itself is always 100%.

If a formula uses 1000 g of flour, 700 g of water, 20 g of salt, and 10 g of yeast, then in baker's percentages that is 100% flour, 70% water, 2% salt, and 1% yeast.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — What is baker's math?](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2022/04/28/what-is-bakers-math-and-how-can-i-use-it-in-my-everyday-bread-baking)*

### Why Bakers Use Percentages Instead of Cups

Percentages are weight-based, which removes the guesswork that comes with volume measures. A cup of flour can vary by 20% or more depending on how it is scooped, but 500 g of flour is always 500 g. Because everything is relative to flour, you can double or halve a formula without redoing the math on each ingredient.

### Reading a Formula at a Glance

Experienced bakers can often predict how a dough will behave just from its percentages. Salt is typically around 1.8-2% of the flour weight; much higher tastes harsh and slows fermentation. Yeast at 1-2% gives a brisk rise, while 0.2-0.5% suits a slow, overnight ferment. A levain or preferment is also given as a percentage of total flour.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — What is baker's math?](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2022/04/28/what-is-bakers-math-and-how-can-i-use-it-in-my-everyday-bread-baking)*

## Hydration

Hydration is the water weight divided by the flour weight, expressed as a percentage. For example, 750 g of water with 1000 g of flour is 75% hydration. It is one of the most important numbers for predicting how a dough feels and what kind of crumb it produces.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Bread hydration, explained](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2023/01/11/bread-hydration)*

### Low vs. High Hydration

Exact cutoffs vary between bakers, but a useful rough guide is:

- **Stiff doughs (roughly 55-65%)**: bagels, pretzels, many sandwich breads. Easy to handle, tight even crumb and chew.
- **Medium (roughly 65-75%)**: most everyday breads and many sourdoughs. A good balance of handling and openness.
- **High (roughly 75-85%+)**: ciabatta, rustic open-crumb sourdough. Sticky and slack, harder to handle, but capable of big irregular holes.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Bread hydration, explained](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2023/01/11/bread-hydration)*

### Why Hydration Changes the Crumb

More water means a more extensible, looser gluten network that can trap larger gas pockets, which tends toward an open, airy crumb. Less water gives a firmer dough that holds a tighter, more uniform structure. Hydration also affects the crust: with good fermentation, wetter doughs often produce a more blistered crust, though they also tend to need longer bakes and can develop a somewhat thicker crust.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — With bread, is wetter better?](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2021/08/16/with-bread-is-wetter-better-high-hydration-dough)*

### Flour Affects How Much Water You Need

Different flours absorb different amounts of water. Whole grain and high-protein bread flours are "thirstier" and drink more than white all-purpose flour, because the bran and germ act like sponges, so the same hydration percentage can feel very different depending on the flour. Treat any stated hydration as a starting point and adjust to the feel of the dough.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Bread hydration, explained](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2023/01/11/bread-hydration)*

## Core Ingredients

Bread can be made from just four things: flour, water, salt, and a leavening agent. Understanding the job each one does makes troubleshooting far easier.

### Flour

Flour provides starch and protein. The proteins glutenin and gliadin link together to form gluten when hydrated and worked, and gluten is the elastic network that traps gas and gives bread its structure. The higher the protein content, the "stronger" the flour and the more gas it can trap, giving a higher rise and more chew.

- **Bread flour**: higher protein (commonly around 12-14%; King Arthur Bread Flour is 12.7%), stronger gluten, more chew and rise. Good default for most yeast breads.
- **All-purpose flour**: moderate protein (commonly around 10-12%; King Arthur all-purpose is 11.7%), versatile, slightly less structure.
- **Whole wheat**: contains bran and germ, more nutritious and flavorful, but the bran cuts gluten strands and makes denser loaves. Often blended with white flour.
- **Rye**: low gluten, sticky, distinctive flavor. Usually used in part, not whole.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Protein percentage in flour: why it matters](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2023/09/25/protein-percentage)*

### Water

Water hydrates the flour, activates enzymes, dissolves salt, and lets gluten develop. Temperature matters: cool water slows fermentation, warm water speeds it up. Most home bakers aim for a final dough temperature around 24-26 C (75-78 F) and adjust water temperature to hit it. Avoid very hot water, which can damage or kill yeast (it begins to suffer around 49 C / 120 F and is killed at about 60 C / 140 F).

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Dough temperature reference](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/pro/reference/dough-temperature)*  
*Source: [Bob's Red Mill — What temperature kills yeast?](https://www.bobsredmill.com/articles/what-temperature-kills-yeast)*

### Salt

Salt is not just for flavor. It tightens and strengthens the gluten network, slows yeast activity (the yeast gives up some water to the salt by osmosis) so fermentation is more even and controllable, and improves crust color and keeping quality by preserving residual sugars for browning. Around 1.8-2% of flour weight is typical. Bread made with no salt tastes flat and ferments unpredictably fast, and the dough is slacker and stickier.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Why is salt important in yeast bread?](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2020/07/29/why-is-salt-important-in-yeast-bread)*

### Yeast

Commercial yeast is a single strain of Saccharomyces cerevisiae that ferments quickly and predictably.

- **Active dry yeast**: traditionally dissolved (proofed) in warm water first, though modern versions are made gently enough that this is no longer necessary and they can be mixed straight in. A touch slower off the mark than instant.
- **Instant (or rapid-rise) yeast**: finer granules, mixes straight into the flour, and rises somewhat faster than active dry. The two can be swapped at roughly a 1:1 ratio.
- **Fresh (cake) yeast**: perishable, used by weight in larger amounts; common in professional settings.

Yeast eats sugars in the flour and produces carbon dioxide (which leavens) and alcohol and acids (which flavor).

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Active dry vs. instant yeast](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2022/08/15/active-dry-versus-instant-yeast)*

### Levain / Sourdough Starter

A sourdough starter (also called a levain when built up for a specific bake) is a living culture of wild yeast and lactic acid bacteria maintained in a mix of flour and water. It leavens bread more slowly than commercial yeast and contributes tangy, complex flavor from the acids the bacteria produce.

- A **starter** is the culture you keep and feed over time.
- A **levain** is a portion of starter built up (fed) shortly before mixing, sized for a particular dough.

Sourdough demands more patience and attention to temperature and timing, but rewards it with flavor, keeping quality, and no need for packaged yeast.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Sourdough baking guide](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/learn/guides/sourdough)*

## Optional Ingredients

Beyond the core four, many breads add enrichments or adjuncts that change texture and flavor.

- **Fats** (butter, oil): tenderize the crumb and extend shelf life; common in sandwich and enriched breads.
- **Sugar / honey**: feed the yeast, add flavor, and promote browning.
- **Eggs and milk**: enrich the dough for soft, rich breads like brioche and milk bread.
- **Seeds and grains**: add flavor, texture, and nutrition.

Enriched doughs (with fat, sugar, and egg) ferment more slowly and brown faster, which is worth remembering when you bake them.

## The High-Level Process

Almost every yeast or sourdough bread follows the same arc. The details and timing vary, but the sequence is remarkably consistent.

### 1. Mix

Combine flour, water, leavening, and salt until no dry flour remains. The goal is even hydration and the beginning of gluten formation. Mixing can be done by hand or machine.

### 2. Autolyse (optional but common)

A rest of flour and water alone (salt and yeast held back, since both tighten gluten), that lets the flour hydrate and gluten begin to form on its own. It makes the dough easier to develop, improves extensibility, and reduces the kneading needed. Twenty minutes to an hour is typical. A liquid levain or preferment can be included since its water is needed to hydrate the flour.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Using the autolyse method](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2017/09/29/autolyse-sourdough)*

### 3. Bulk Fermentation

The first and longest rise, where the whole mass of dough ferments together. Yeast produces gas, the dough grows and gains strength, and flavor develops. During bulk, bakers often perform stretch-and-folds to build structure. This stage ends when the dough is noticeably risen, smoother than at the start, airy, and jiggly, with visible bubbles; a 30-75% increase in volume is a common target, with the right number depending on dough temperature and style. Judge it by these signs rather than the clock.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Bulk fermentation, explained](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2019/07/22/bulk-fermentation)*

### 4. Shape

The dough is divided (if making multiple loaves), pre-shaped, rested briefly, then shaped into its final form. Shaping builds surface tension, which helps the loaf hold its form and rise upward rather than spreading.

### 5. Proof

The final rise after shaping, in a banneton, tin, or on a tray. The dough relaxes and inflates one more time. Proofing can happen at room temperature or, for flavor and scheduling, slowly in the refrigerator (a cold retard). The poke test helps judge readiness.

### 6. Bake

Heat sets the structure: the dough puffs dramatically in the first minutes (oven spring), the starches gelatinize, the crust forms and browns, and the interior sets. Steam early in the bake keeps the crust soft and pliable long enough for full expansion; venting it and the high heat then dry and color the crust.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — Baking bread with steam](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2017/02/28/baking-bread-with-steam)*

### 7. Cool

Bread continues to cook and set as it cools. Cutting too early releases trapped steam before the crumb has set and can leave it gummy. Most lean loaves benefit from at least an hour (often 1-2 hours) on a wire rack before slicing.

*Source: [King Arthur Baking — How to tell if bread is done](https://www.kingarthurbaking.com/blog/2023/05/31/how-to-tell-if-bread-is-done)*

## Putting It Together

Once you internalize that bread is flour and water given structure by gluten, leavened by yeast or wild culture, seasoned and controlled by salt, and transformed by heat, every recipe becomes a set of variations on the same theme. Baker's percentages and hydration give you the vocabulary; the process gives you the rhythm.
